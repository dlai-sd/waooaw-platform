# Case 002 — Sana Mirza, Beauty Artist

**Type:** Constitutional Discovery Case

**Status:** Active

**Purpose:** Stress-test the Constitution against a sole-proprietor creative business where the customer's personal brand IS the service. Discover constitutional seams distinct from Case 001.

**Business:** Sana Mirza, Bridal and Occasion Makeup Artist, Bandra, Mumbai

---

## The World Before WAOOAW

Sana Mirza is a freelance makeup artist based in Bandra, Mumbai. She has been working professionally for six years. She does not have a studio. She travels to clients or works from a home setup for consultations. Her business is herself.

She specializes in bridal, engagement, and occasion makeup. October through December is her peak — wedding season. January through March is unpredictable and often thin.

Her current reality:

- 2,400 Instagram followers, built over three years of posting work photographs
- She posts when she has something beautiful to show — approximately 3-4 times per week during peak season, once a week or less in lean months
- She has no Facebook presence worth mentioning
- She receives most bookings through word of mouth and Instagram DMs
- She has no system for tracking inquiries or responding consistently

Her competitive environment:

- Dozens of makeup artists in Mumbai at every price point
- Instagram is the primary discovery channel for her category
- Clients choose based on aesthetic fit — they spend hours browsing portfolios
- Pricing varies enormously; Sana is in the mid-to-premium range

Her current business concern, January 2025:

Wedding season has just ended. She has three confirmed bookings for February and nothing confirmed for March. Last January she had nine. She does not understand the decline. She posts good work. She replies to inquiries. Things used to come naturally.

Her constraints:

- Budget: INR 6,000–10,000 per month if results justify it
- Time: She has time between assignments but does not know what to do with it
- Expertise: She understands Instagram aesthetics instinctively — better than any tool
- Trust: Moderate skepticism. She tried a social media scheduling tool last year. It made her feed look generic. She deleted it after three weeks.

Her primary concern about any help:

> "I can't have someone posting on my behalf if it doesn't sound like me. My clients follow me for my voice, not just my work."

---

## Tuesday, 2:00 PM — Discovery

Sana is scrolling Instagram during a slow afternoon.

She sees a Reel. It is not an advertisement in the traditional sense. It is a case study — a before-and-after story of a Mumbai-based home baker who hired a "Digital Marketing Professional" and grew her inquiry rate by 60% in 90 days. The narration in the Reel is clean and specific. It mentions WAOOAW.

She watches it twice.

She is skeptical. She has seen many tools promise results. But something is different about this one. The baker in the video is not talking about AI. She is talking about hiring someone. She says: *"I set the goals. The professional learned my voice. I reviewed everything. Now I barely think about it."*

Sana searches WAOOAW.

**Constitutional layer active:**

No rights have been activated. Sana is not a customer.

The institution is in observational state only.

**Note:** WAOOAW has reached Sana through its own Digital Marketing Professional — Acceptance Scenario 004. The institution is marketing itself using its own methodology. This is the first case where the platform's constitutional model directly influenced discovery.

## Tuesday, 4:30 PM — Evaluation

Sana looks at the Digital Marketing Professional profiles available for beauty and creative businesses.

She has a specific concern before anything else: aesthetic compatibility.

She types into a search: *"makeup artist Mumbai"* and looks at the Professional Experience Ledger summary of one profile.

She sees: "23 beauty industry engagements. Instagram content for salon, makeup, skincare clients."

She thinks: *but do they understand MY aesthetic?*

She reads further. She notices something unexpected. The profile says:

*"This professional does not generate your visual identity. It learns and extends the aesthetic you have already built. You own the creative standard. The professional operates within it."*

She stops scrolling.

That sentence changes her evaluation entirely. This is not a tool replacing her. It is something learning to assist her on her own terms.

She continues reading. She finds the section on what requires her approval:

- Every post caption before publishing
- Every image selection or template before use
- Any hashtag strategy change
- Any promotional content

She finds the section on what the professional will never do without asking:

- Post content that does not match her approved visual style
- Tag clients without explicit permission
- Publish before-and-after images of any client
- Respond to enquiries on her behalf
- Change her bio or profile information

She reads the last point again. *Before-and-after images require explicit permission.*

She thinks: *someone thought about this.*

She clicks "Hire."

**Constitutional observation:** Sana's decision to hire was triggered not by capability claims but by a constitutional boundary statement — the promise that her creative identity is not replaced but extended. The institution's value proposition, at this moment, is constitutional. It is about what the professional will not do.

**Constitutional observation:** The prohibition on client photographs without permission surfaces pre-employment. This is CP-001 operating correctly — rights and limits visible before contract formation.

## Tuesday, 5:15 PM — Hiring

The hiring configuration for a beauty artist is different from a dental clinic.

Sana is asked:

1. What is your primary business goal? She selects: *Increase client enquiries.*
2. What does success look like in 90 days? She types: *"Consistent inquiries every week, not just during wedding season."*
3. What is your visual style? She is shown three descriptors and asked to select up to three: *Soft, Natural, Editorial, Bold, Dramatic, Minimal, Warm, Modern.* She selects: Soft, Natural, Warm.
4. Upload three posts that best represent your aesthetic. She uploads three.
5. Which platforms? She selects: Instagram only for now.
6. Do you want to review all captions before publishing? She selects: Yes, always.
7. Do you want to review image templates before use? She selects: Yes, for the first 60 days.

She is shown the Employment Contract draft.

She reads it carefully. She notices one clause she did not expect:

*"The Digital Professional's authority to post does not include the authority to define your brand. Brand definition remains exclusively with the customer. Any content the professional produces must conform to the aesthetic standards you have established. The professional has no authority to introduce new visual directions without your explicit approval."*

She reads it again.

She signs.

**Constitutional event recorded in Constitutional Audit Ledger:**
- Employment Contract formed
- Professional Identity BMP-01147 (Beauty Marketing Professional, Creative Industries Specialization)
- Authority License: Level 1 — Observation and Draft Only
- Creative Standard: Customer-defined. Three reference posts registered as aesthetic anchors.
- Customer Evidence Ledger: Created, empty
- Professional Experience Ledger: Updated — new employment entry

**Rights now active:**
- All standard Customer Rights from Article IX
- Additional: Customer's creative identity is constitutionally protected from professional override

**New constitutional observation:** Case 001 established Customer Rights around actions (override, terminate, audit). This case surfaces a new right not explicitly named in the Constitution: the right to define the creative standard that governs professional output. The professional may not introduce creative directions. The customer's aesthetic is a constitutional boundary, not merely a preference setting.

**Constitutional Discovery CD-006 identified:** The Constitution does not explicitly define creative identity as a protected customer right. It must.

## Week 1 — Aesthetic Onboarding

The professional does not immediately begin producing content.

It spends the first week in what Case 001 called the observation period — but here, the observation is aesthetic, not analytical.

It studies Sana's three reference posts. It studies her entire Instagram feed. It identifies:

- Average caption length: 47 words
- Tone: personal, warm, occasionally poetic
- Common phrases: *"glowing," "she felt like herself," "the most beautiful version"*
- Image characteristics: natural light, warm tones, close portraits
- Hashtag patterns: mix of specific (#mumbaibride) and aspirational (#bridalmakeup)

At the end of week one, the professional sends Sana a document: **Creative Standard Profile.**

It says: *"Based on studying your feed, I've created a draft creative standard that will govern all content I produce. Please review, correct, and approve before I begin drafting. If I have misunderstood your voice, this is the moment to correct it."*

Sana reads it. She makes four corrections. She approves it.

**Constitutional observation:** The professional has externalized and made legible the aesthetic standard before producing any content. This is constitutionally significant: the creative standard is now explicit, auditable, and owned by the customer. It is evidence in the Customer Evidence Ledger. If any future content drifts from it, there is a constitutional reference point.

This is not a product feature. It is constitutional behavior.

## Week 2 — First Caption Draft

The professional sends its first draft caption for an existing portfolio photo Sana selects.

Draft: *"Soft and luminous — her bridal morning, beautifully remembered. 🌸 Enquiries open for 2025. DM to book your date."*

Sana reads it. She replies: *"Good. But I never use flower emojis. And 'luminous' isn't my word — change to 'lit from within'."*

The professional updates the draft. It also records: *Customer does not use flower emojis. Preferred vocabulary includes 'lit from within' over 'luminous'.*

Sana approves the revised caption.

**Constitutional observation:** The rejection and correction is not a failure. It is the Trust Cycle operating. The professional is learning. The evidence produced by this correction — vocabulary preferences, emoji restrictions — is stored in the Customer Evidence Ledger and refines the Creative Standard Profile. The customer's creative identity is being made more precise through evidence, not less.

## Week 3 — First Constitutional Seam: The Overlap Problem

On a Thursday afternoon, Sana spontaneously photographs a stunning eye look she is working on for a client consultation. It is striking. She posts it herself on Instagram without thinking — she has done this for years.

Two hours later, she receives a draft from the professional: a caption for the same image, which the professional had queued after seeing the photo in her camera uploads.

The professional and the customer have both acted on the same content moment.

No post was published twice. But Sana realizes: the professional was about to post the same image she just posted. If she had not acted first, the professional would have.

She messages the professional: *"I posted that photo myself. I didn't think about it."*

The professional responds: *"Noted. I have removed the draft from the queue. Going forward, if you post content independently, please mark it in the dashboard so I can track it as customer-originated. This helps me avoid duplication and keeps your evidence records accurate."*

**Constitutional observation:** The institution has no formal protocol for concurrent engagement — when both the customer and the professional act on the same channel. The professional handled this gracefully, but the handling was improvised, not constitutional.

**Constitutional Discovery CD-007 identified:** The Employment Contract does not define protocol for concurrent customer and professional engagement on the same channel. When both parties are active, there is no constitutional clarity about:
1. Who has priority on content scheduling
2. How customer-originated posts are classified in evidence
3. Whether the professional's queue should pause when the customer is actively posting

This must be resolved before architecture designs the content scheduling system.

## Week 5 — A Client Asks About Privacy

A client who had a bridal makeup session three weeks earlier messages Sana:

*"Hi Sana, I saw you have a new marketing person helping you. Are my photos being used anywhere? I'm a bit private."*

Sana is momentarily uncertain. She checks her dashboard.

She sees that the professional has never posted that client's photos — they were never in any draft queue. The professional has adhered to its constitutional constraint: no before-and-after images without explicit permission.

But the client's question reveals something important. The professional has seen the photos during the observation period — they were in Sana's feed archives and shared folders. The client does not know that a digital professional has accessed historical content.

Sana messages the platform: *"My client wants to know if her photos were used. Can I confirm that?"*

The platform surfaces the Customer Evidence Ledger for that content item: Observed (read), Not queued, Not published.

Sana replies to her client: *"Your photos have not been used in any posts and are not planned for use. You're fully protected."*

The client is satisfied.

**Constitutional observation:** The platform correctly produced evidence that protected the customer's ability to answer a third-party privacy concern. However, a deeper constitutional question surfaced: the professional accessed historical content during onboarding, and that access was not explicitly consented to by the individuals featured in that content.

**Constitutional Discovery CD-008 identified:** The Employment Contract establishes data access between customer and professional. But the customer's archive may contain third-party data — clients, subjects, collaborators — whose consent was not obtained for processing by an autonomous digital professional. The Constitution's three-ledger model addresses customer data ownership but does not address third-party data embedded in customer archives.

This is a constitutional gap with genuine privacy implications. It may require a new constitutional principle or amendment.

## Month 2 — Creative Voice Drift

After six weeks of collaboration, Sana begins to notice something subtle.

The captions are technically correct. They follow the Creative Standard Profile. But they feel slightly off. They are professional without being personal.

She cannot articulate it precisely. She tells the professional:

*"The captions are fine. But they don't feel like me. Something is missing. I feel like I'm reading a version of myself, not myself."*

The professional responds: *"Thank you for naming this. Can you share one of your own captions from before we started, and one I've written recently? I'd like to understand where the difference is."*

Sana sends two examples.

The professional identifies the difference: her own writing contains grammatical imperfections that feel intentional — sentence fragments, ellipses, deliberate pauses. The Creative Standard Profile captured vocabulary and tone but not cadence and rhythm.

The professional updates the Creative Standard Profile with new examples. It asks Sana to review three revised captions.

She reads them. One still feels off. Two feel right.

She approves the two. She marks one for revision with notes.

**Constitutional observation:** The Creative Standard Profile is a living document. It must be updated as the customer's aesthetic understanding deepens through the engagement. This is not failure — it is the Trust Cycle operating. Evidence produces learning. Learning refines the standard. The standard governs future output.

**But this also surfaces something structurally important:** Creative voice drift is not a constitutional violation. The professional operated within the approved standard. But the standard was insufficiently defined at the time of approval. This means the customer's initial approval of the Creative Standard Profile was not fully informed consent — she did not know what she did not know about her own voice until she saw it imperfectly reproduced.

**Constitutional Discovery CD-009 identified:** Informed consent to creative standards requires a calibration period before the standard is treated as constitutional. A creative standard approved at Week 1 is a hypothesis, not a settled agreement. The institution needs a formal calibration period — a defined window during which the Creative Standard Profile is explicitly provisional, and corrections do not constitute rejection of work. Only after the calibration period concludes does the approved standard become the binding reference.

## Month 3 — Renewal With Conditions

Three months after hiring, the renewal prompt arrives.

Sana's inquiry rate has improved. She went from 3 confirmed bookings in February to 9 in April. She attributes approximately 4 of those to Instagram activity the professional managed.

She is satisfied with the results but still has the creative voice concern in mind.

She renews. She adds two conditions:

1. The Creative Standard Profile must be reviewed and re-approved every 30 days for the next three months.
2. She wants the option to write her own captions and have the professional handle scheduling only, for certain posts she designates.

The professional confirms both conditions can be accommodated within the current authority model.

**Constitutional observation:** The renewal conditions reveal a new authority configuration: the customer wants the professional to execute in some areas while she retains full creative control in others. The authority model in Case 001 was simpler — approve or reject complete actions. Here the customer is splitting creative and operational authority within the same professional scope.

**Constitutional Discovery CD-010 identified:** The Employment Contract does not currently support split authority — where the customer provides the creative input and delegates only the operational execution to the professional. The architecture needs to support: `Customer-Originated Content + Professional-Executed Distribution` as a distinct authority mode.

## Month 4 — Peak Season Approach

August arrives. Sana begins receiving organic inquiries for the October-December wedding season.

The professional flags an opportunity: *"Wedding season is 6 weeks away. Based on your inquiry pattern from last year, this is the optimal time to begin targeted outreach content. I'd recommend shifting posting frequency from 3x per week to 5x per week for the next six weeks. This would require your approval for all content but would increase visibility during the discovery window. Would you like to discuss this?"*

Sana says yes.

The professional produces a 6-week content calendar — 30 post drafts, organized by theme, with captions in her voice, placeholder image slots.

Sana reviews it over two evenings. She approves 24, rejects 4, and modifies 2.

She notes: the modified ones were posts that featured promotional language she had not approved. The professional had included a *"Book your date before spots fill"* call to action on three posts.

**Constitutional observation:** The professional introduced promotional urgency language that touches the scope boundary from Case 001 (CP-003). It did not produce a scope-boundary confirmation request.

**Seam tested:** Customer ↔ Professional — CP-003 re-exercised.

**Constitutional observation:** CP-003 should have triggered a mandatory scope-boundary confirmation for the promotional language. It did not. This is a constitutional consistency failure — not a new discovery, but a reminder that precedents must be architecturally enforced, not merely expected as professional behavior.

**Precedent re-affirmed:** CP-003 is not optional. Architecture must enforce it.

## Month 6 — Exit: Voluntary Non-Renewal

At the six-month mark, Sana decides not to renew.

Not because of dissatisfaction with results. Results are positive.

She decides not to renew because wedding season is ending and she is entering her lean period. She does not want to pay for a professional during months when she will be posting infrequently anyway.

She plans to rehire in August for the next season.

**Constitutional event:**
- Employment Contract: Not renewed by Customer
- Customer Evidence Ledger: Retained by Customer. Not deleted.
- Professional Experience Ledger: Engagement recorded — 6 months, Beauty, Bridal, Mumbai, Creative Industries
- Constitutional Audit Ledger: Non-renewal event recorded
- Authority License BMP-01147: Suspended for this engagement

**Constitutional observation:** Non-renewal is not termination. The customer leaves the door open. The institution must support re-engagement without requiring the customer to start from zero. The Creative Standard Profile, the aesthetic calibration, the vocabulary record — all of this should be available if Sana returns.

**Constitutional Discovery CD-011 identified:** The Employment Contract lifecycle does not distinguish between termination (relationship ended) and suspension (relationship paused). For seasonal businesses, the institution needs a Suspension state — where the Employment Contract is paused, the Customer Evidence Ledger is preserved, and re-engagement can resume from the last known state. Requiring the customer to re-onboard from scratch on seasonal return is constitutionally wasteful and economically destructive.

## Constitutional Discoveries in This Case

**CD-006 — Creative Identity as Protected Customer Right**

The Constitution does not explicitly name creative identity as a customer right. For sole-proprietor businesses where the customer's personal brand IS the service, the professional's scope must be constitutionally bounded by the customer's creative identity, not merely by capability limits.

*Architectural implication:* The Constitution requires amendment or a new precedent establishing creative identity as a constitutionally protected customer asset. Architecture must support a Creative Standard Profile as a constitutional document owned by the customer.

**CD-007 — Concurrent Engagement Protocol**

When both customer and professional are actively engaged on the same channel, there is no constitutional protocol governing priority, duplication prevention, or evidence classification.

*Architectural implication:* Employment Contract must define concurrent engagement rules. Architecture needs a content state that distinguishes: Customer-Originated, Professional-Originated, and Collaborative.

**CD-008 — Third-Party Data in Customer Archives**

Professional onboarding involves accessing historical customer content that may contain identifiable third-party individuals who did not consent to processing by an autonomous digital professional.

*Architectural implication:* The three-ledger model must be extended or supplemented to address third-party data rights within customer archives. This may require a constitutional amendment.

**CD-009 — Creative Standard Calibration Period**

A creative standard approved at Week 1 is a hypothesis, not settled informed consent. Creative professionals require a formal calibration window before the Creative Standard Profile becomes a binding constitutional reference.

*Architectural implication:* Employment Contract must include a Creative Calibration Period — a defined window where the standard is provisional and refinements do not constitute approval violations.

**CD-010 — Split Authority Mode**

The current authority model treats professional actions as complete units. Some customers require split authority — customer provides creative input, professional executes distribution. This is not accommodated in the current employment model.

*Architectural implication:* Authority model must support Customer-Created, Professional-Distributed as a first-class execution mode.

**CD-011 — Suspension vs Termination**

Non-renewal is not termination. Seasonal businesses require the ability to pause employment without losing the institutional memory accumulated during the engagement.

*Architectural implication:* Employment Contract lifecycle must support three states: Active, Suspended, Terminated. Suspended contracts preserve all evidence, standards, and calibration. Re-activation resumes from the last known state.

## What This Case Confirms About the Constitution

The First Law held: Trust was earned through evidence, not assumed. Sana did not trust generic AI content. She trusted a professional that learned and submitted to her creative standard.

The Second Law held: Authority over creative direction was never taken by the professional. It was always returned to the customer.

The Three Systems held: Capability, Trust, and Authority operated independently. The professional was capable of producing content (Capability) before Sana fully trusted it (Trust) and before it had authority to post without review (Authority).

CP-001 held: Rights and limits were surfaced before the contract was signed. The prohibition on client photos before employment influenced Sana's decision to hire.

CP-003 was violated once: the promotional language scope-boundary confirmation was not triggered. This confirms that CP-003 must be architecturally enforced, not reliant on professional behavior.

**No constitutional contradiction was discovered under the conditions exercised by this case.**

Six constitutional discoveries were made. All registered in PRECEDENTS.md.
